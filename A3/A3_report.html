Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The spiral shape makes it very easy to compare trends over the years. It also allows us to see trends over certain seasons. For instance there seems to be peaks during winters of 2021 and 2022.
  • The spiral shape as it moves outwards with time, also reflects the metaphor of the pandemic unfolding which I think is very neat.
  • It is clear that big width means a lot of cases. That increase in size is clear in January 2022 which help corroborate the story of the NYTimes article.

Design Critique:

  • This visualization is intended for the general public to give them a sense of what to expect with a new variant of covid, i.e. omicron, on the loose. While the trend of increasing cases during the time of omicron (Jan 2022), there are a lot of unclear factors. Things like 'how much are the number of cases increasing by', 'what other external factors might be at play here', are not answered. I'll dive into some of these below.
  • The number of covid cases are depicted as area. However, as humans we are not very good at gauging differences in areas. Additionally, the area in this visualization is not aligned with any scale to help with comparison. To address this, one could use length instead of area to depict the number of cases and use either colour or discrete units along the length to help the audience easily understand the number of cases without too much cognitive load.
  • From the figure, the legend claims ‘150K cases’ and there is an annotation that marks a ‘7-day average’. I found this information rather vague and confusing. I would assume that they are referring to the number of active covid cases in the graph and perhaps some slice of the area depicts a 7-day average of this value. However since the area in the visualization is continuous and not discrete, it is not clear what the size of this “slice” is. To address this point, adding some kind of discreteness to the plot along the spiral will help people understand how many days one unit refers to.
  • Given that this visualization is aimed for the general public, I worry that there is not enough context packed into the visualization. In addition to the need for clarity in the legend and annotation discussed above, I think there needs to be more information such as - “when did the delta virus outbreak happen”, “what are the number of hospitalizations and/or deaths”, “what are the number of vaccinations” and so on. The story of the article seems to be that the omicron variant is different from previous variants in ways that people may get infected more easily but the disease won’t be as fatal as before. Additionally, getting vaccinated is encouraged. To drive these points home, I think the other contexts listed above would really help.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For this visualization my goal was to showcase the trends of deaths and number of covid cases while also highlighting the context of delta and omicron variants in the visualization. I liked my sketch 3 and started with that as an inspiration for this visualization. Data Cleaning: During preliminary analysis of the data, I found that the cumulative number of cases suddenly dropped on two dates, one in September 2022 and one in December 2022. I excluded these data points. Additionally I found that there were some dates with negative number of new cases or number of deceased people. I excluded all of these rows with a filter. Design rationale: I was really set on working with the spiral shape because I think it really highlights the yearly trends and showcases the passage of time very nicely. In order to do this I calculated the (x,y) cartesian coordinates from polar coordinates where the angle is determined by the date, and the radius of length is determined by either the number of cases or the number of deaths on that day. The scales of the two values (number of cases and deaths) is quite varied and thus it was difficult for me to find a way to overlap the representations without creating confusion based on the magnitude of the markings (due to different scales). Thus I decided to place both spirals side by side to allow for easy visual comparison. Additionally, I added the same annotation for omicron and delta outbreaks to provide context for both plots. Due to a lack of skill and time, I was unable to figure out how to plot discrete blocks (as in sketch 3) instead of bars. Since unaligned bars (as present in the spiral plot) are difficult to measure, I decided to use colour also to depict respective magnitudes. I tried to use discrete colour blocks on the scale to visually help people easily understand the values in the visualization. Insights and story: It is interesting to see that there is always a spike in number of deaths a little after there is a spike in number of cases (as seen in Oct 2020, Aug 2021 and Jan 2022). Additionally, two of these spikes correspond to the delta and omicron outbreaks. We also see that the number of deaths don’t spike as much as the number of cases in Jan 2022, i.e. with the omicron variant. This makes sense because the variant was known to be less fatal and people were more likely to be vaccinated at this time. Another insight that I thought was really interesting is that the COVID vaccines seem to came out during a relatively high number of cases. The number of deaths associated with the wave of these COVID cases is very high and hits the peak number of deaths. After the vaccine the number of deaths doesn’t ever go that high despite other waves of COVID.

Reflection

Critique: I think I may have been too tied to the spiral design from the beginning of the design process. My lack of tableau skills did limit the kinds of visual encodings I could use within the spiral visualization. For example, I wanted to use discrete blocks similar to a unit chart (as seen in sketch 3) but I couldn’t figure out how to visualize that. Using discrete blocks to indicate a granularity of time and number of cases would make it much easier to read the chart, however I couldn’t address it in this visualization. Alternatively a unit chart with the number of cases (covid confirmed cases or deaths) with 3 rows, one for each year would have allowed for the seasonal wise comparison and given me more freedom with visual encodings because of the chart’s relative popularity and my comfort with it. I wonder if this would have made the plot easier to read as well instead of the spiral. I like the use of visual symbols to indicate different contextual events that help communicate the story with the audience. The use of colour also helps the user understand the values more clearly.
